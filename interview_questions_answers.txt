SECTION A: GENERAL EMBEDDED & ELECTRONICS
==========================================


Q1 - Project Based
-------------------
Describe an embedded system you built end to end. What microcontroller did you use,
what did it control, and what was the hardest technical problem you solved?

EVALUATION NOTES (not for candidate):
No single correct answer. A good answer includes:
- Specific MCU (not just "Arduino")
- Real constraints they designed around (power, timing, size)
- A genuine hard problem, not "setting up I2C was tricky"
- What they would do differently, shows self-awareness
Red flag: Vague answers like "I built a smart home system using ESP32 and it worked great"


Q2 - Circuit Design
--------------------
You need to switch a 12V inductive load using a 3.3V microcontroller GPIO.
Describe the complete circuit you would design, including every protection
component and why it is there.

ANSWER:
Complete circuit:

3.3V GPIO -- [100 ohm gate resistor] --> MOSFET gate (e.g. IRLZ44N, logic level)
                                          MOSFET source --> GND
                                          MOSFET drain --> Load negative
                                          Load positive --> 12V
                                          1N4007 flyback diode across load (cathode to 12V)

Component reasoning:

Logic-level MOSFET:
Standard MOSFET needs 10V gate drive and will not fully turn on at 3.3V.
IRLZ44N turns on fully at 3.3V. Using a standard MOSFET here causes it to
operate in the linear region, generating excessive heat and not switching properly.

Gate resistor (100 ohm):
Limits inrush current into gate capacitance, damps oscillation, and slows the
switching edge to reduce EMI. Without it the gate can ring and cause unpredictable
switching behaviour.

Flyback diode:
Inductive load stores energy in its magnetic field. When the MOSFET switches off,
that energy must go somewhere. Without a diode it appears as a large voltage spike
on the drain, which destroys the MOSFET. The diode clamps this spike and safely
dissipates energy back into the supply.

Gate pull-down resistor (10k ohm to GND, optional but recommended):
Ensures the MOSFET stays off when the GPIO is floating during MCU boot or reset.
Without this the load can briefly switch on at power-up.


Q3 - Debugging: Hardware
-------------------------
You bring up a new PCB for the first time. The microcontroller is unresponsive.
No serial output, no LED, no response to flash attempts. Walk through your
debugging process step by step.

ANSWER:

Step 1 - Measure power rails
Multimeter on all VDD pins. Is the correct voltage present? If 0V, trace back to
the regulator. If lower than expected, the board may have a short drawing excess current.

Step 2 - Check for shorts
Power off. Measure resistance between VDD and GND. Should be several kilohms minimum.
Near 0 ohm means solder bridge or damaged component. Check visually under magnification.

Step 3 - Verify crystal and clock
Oscilloscope on crystal pins. If there is no oscillation, the MCU will not run.
Check load capacitors are correct value and placed correctly.

Step 4 - Check BOOT pins
On STM32, the BOOT0 pin determines boot mode. If BOOT0 is floating or HIGH, the MCU
boots into bootloader, not your firmware. Check the boot pin state and pull-down resistor.

Step 5 - Check NRST
NRST should be HIGH during normal operation. If stuck LOW, the MCU is held in reset.
Check the reset circuit and any external reset devices.

Step 6 - Try connecting debugger
Attempt to connect via SWD using ST-Link or J-Link. If the debugger sees the chip,
the MCU is alive and the problem is firmware. If no connection at all, the fault is hardware.

Step 7 - Visual inspection under magnification
Look for solder bridges, tombstoned components, backwards ICs, missing bypass capacitors.
These are the most common causes of first-bring-up failures.


Q4 - Debugging: Firmware
-------------------------
A sensor is returning correct values in testing but randomly returns garbage
in the final product. Nothing in the code changed. What do you investigate?

ANSWER:

Hardware causes:

Power supply noise:
The bench supply is clean. The real product has switching noise from motors and relays
that couples into the ADC reference or sensor supply rail. This causes erratic readings
that never appear on the bench.

Ground loops:
Different ground potentials between the sensor and the MCU in the final machine wiring.
Even a few millivolts of ground offset corrupts ADC readings.

Long cable acting as antenna:
In the real environment, cable picks up EMI from motors and switching circuits.
This does not occur on the bench with short cables.

Signal voltage exceeding ADC input range:
Causes latch-up or erratic readings. Check that no transient on the signal line
exceeds the MCU supply voltage.

Firmware causes:

Interrupt collision:
Another interrupt fires mid-conversion and corrupts the result buffer.
Check interrupt priorities and ensure ADC result registers are read atomically.

DMA misconfiguration:
DMA writing to wrong memory location under certain timing conditions.
Happens when buffer sizes or addresses are set up incorrectly.

Missing volatile keyword:
Compiler optimises away reads of a variable that is being updated by an interrupt.
Any variable shared between main code and an ISR must be declared volatile.

Buffer overflow:
Adjacent memory corrupted by another process overwriting the sensor buffer.

How to isolate:
Replicate real conditions on the bench. Same cable lengths, same power supply,
run motors and other loads nearby. Add logging to capture the exact conditions
when garbage values appear.


Q5 - Communication Protocols
------------------------------
You have a device communicating over UART that intermittently sends corrupted data.
List every possible cause you can think of and how you would isolate the real one.

ANSWER:

Baud rate mismatch:
Scope the line. Measure the width of one bit in microseconds and calculate the
actual baud rate. Compare to both ends.

TX and RX swapped:
Check wiring. With a scope, confirm activity appears on the RX pin when the remote
device is transmitting.

Missing or wrong ground reference:
Measure voltage between GND of both boards. Should be 0V. Any difference corrupts
the logic levels.

Voltage level mismatch (3.3V vs 5V):
Measure logic HIGH voltage on the receiving pin. 5V into a 3.3V input can damage
the pin and causes erratic behaviour.

Cable too long or capacitance rounding edges:
Scope the RX pin close to the receiver. If rising and falling edges are rounded and
slow, signal integrity is the issue. Shorten cable or add series termination.

EMI from nearby switching:
Check if corruption correlates with motor movement or relay switching events.
If yes, add shielding, twisted pair cable, or ferrite beads.

Missing pull-up on open-drain line:
Measure idle state. Should be HIGH. If floating or LOW when idle, pull-up is missing.

Software buffer overflow:
Log exactly when corruption occurs. If it only happens at high data rates or when
the host is busy, the receive buffer is overflowing before data is read.

Parity or stop bit mismatch:
Check configuration on both ends. A single bit difference causes every frame to
be flagged as an error.

Interrupt priority conflict:
If corruption only occurs during specific operations such as motor moves or sensor
reads, a higher-priority interrupt is delaying the UART interrupt and causing
buffer overrun.


Q6 - Thermal
-------------
A component on your board is running hotter than expected under load.
How do you diagnose how hot it actually is, find the cause, and fix it?

ANSWER:

Measuring actual temperature:
- Thermocouple or IR thermometer held against the component body
- Thermal camera if available, best for finding hot spots across the whole board
- NTC thermistor taped to the component surface as a low-cost alternative

Finding the cause:
Calculate expected power dissipation first.
For MOSFETs: P = I squared x Rds(on)
For linear regulators: P = (Vin - Vout) x I

Compare calculated to actual. If the component is much hotter than the calculation
predicts, look for unexpected current paths, wrong component placed, or the component
operating in linear region instead of saturation.

Check whether temperature stabilises or keeps rising. Stabilising means steady-state
thermal resistance issue. Keep rising means thermal runaway or unexpected power source.

Check airflow. Even a small fan or vent changes component temperature significantly.

Fixes in order of preference:

1. Reduce power dissipation: use a switching topology instead of linear, or select a
   component with lower Rds(on) or lower dropout voltage.
2. Improve thermal path: add thermal pad, heatsink, or thermal vias to copper pours.
3. Add airflow: direct a fan at the component.
4. Spread copper on PCB: large ground and power pours act as passive heatsinks.
5. Derate the component: if running near its rating, select a higher-rated part.


Q7 - PCB Design
----------------
What are the most common mistakes engineers make on their first few PCB designs?
What rules do you follow for mixed signal boards with both power and digital circuits?

ANSWER:

Common first-timer mistakes:

Decoupling capacitors too far from MCU power pins:
They stop working above a few megahertz if the trace is longer than a few millimetres.
Must be physically as close as possible, within 1mm ideally.

No ground plane:
Without a solid ground plane, return currents take random paths through the board
and create noise everywhere. Almost every board should have a solid ground plane.

Mixing analog and digital ground incorrectly:
They should be separate pours that meet at exactly one point near the power supply.
Joining them at multiple points creates ground loops.

Connector footprints wrong:
Most common reason for board respins. Always verify connector pinout and footprint
against the physical part before ordering.

No test points:
Impossible to debug a board without probe access. Add test points on every key signal,
power rail, and ground.

Via in pad without filling:
Solder wicks into the via during reflow and leaves insufficient solder on the pad.
Either avoid vias in pads or specify filled and plated vias in fabrication notes.

Silkscreen over pads:
Causes soldering issues. Keep silkscreen clear of all pad areas.

Rules for mixed signal boards:

Separate analog and digital power planes. Connect them at one star point near the
power entry, not at multiple locations.

Keep analog traces away from switching signals such as clocks, PWM lines, and motor
drive traces. Even short parallel runs couple noise.

Calculate trace width for all high-current paths. Use at least 1mm for 2A on 1oz
copper. Do not rely on defaults.

Place 100nF decoupling capacitors on every MCU VDD pin, as close as physically possible.

Stepper driver thermal pads must connect to large copper pours with thermal vias
to inner copper layers. This is the primary thermal path.

Keep step and direction signal traces away from motor coil traces.
Motor lines switch fast and inject noise into adjacent signals.


Q8 - Case Study
----------------
A motion control system works perfectly on the bench but misses steps randomly
when installed in the final machine. No changes were made to firmware or hardware.
What are the possible causes and how do you find the real one?

ANSWER:

The key observation is that bench performance is perfect but the installed machine
is not. Focus entirely on what is different in the installed environment.

Electrical noise from machine wiring:
Long motor cables in the machine act as antennas and couple switching noise into
step, direction, and endstop signal lines. This does not occur on the bench with
short cables in a clean environment.
Test: scope step and direction lines while the machine is running. Look for noise
spikes that could be interpreted as false pulses.

Power supply voltage drop under full load:
The bench PSU is well-regulated. The machine PSU may sag when all axes run
simultaneously and other loads are present.
Test: measure PSU output voltage with all machine loads active during a print.
Compare to voltage measured on the bench.

Mechanical binding or increased load:
The installed machine has more friction due to weight, alignment differences, or
temperature effects. The motor loses steps because it cannot produce enough torque.
Test: turn the axis by hand in both conditions. Does it feel stiffer in the machine?
Temporarily increase motor current by 10 percent and check if steps are still lost.

Vibration causing endstop false triggers:
Machine vibration during moves can cause an endstop switch to momentarily trigger.
Marlin and similar firmware treat this as a real endstop event and stop or reset
the axis position.
Test: check if missed steps correlate with high-speed moves or direction changes
where vibration is highest. Add debounce filtering to endstop inputs.

Motor driver overheating:
In the enclosed machine there is less airflow than on the open bench. Drivers heat
up over time, trigger thermal protection, reduce current output, and steps are lost.
Test: measure driver temperature after running for ten minutes in the machine.
Compare to bench temperature.

Ground loops through machine frame:
The metal chassis of the machine connects circuit grounds through multiple paths
at different potentials. This creates noise on signal lines.
Test: measure voltage between ground points at different locations in the machine.
Add a single-point ground connection between all boards and chassis.

Q1 - STM32 Microcontrollers
-----------------------------
You are configuring a UART peripheral on an STM32 microcontroller. What registers
or settings do you need to configure, and what are the most common mistakes that
cause it to not work on first try?

ANSWER:

What must be configured:

Clock enable:
Enable the USART peripheral clock via the RCC register before writing to any USART
register. If the clock is not enabled, writes to registers are silently ignored.

GPIO alternate function:
TX and RX pins must be set to alternate function mode with the correct AF number.
For example, AF7 is used for USART1 on STM32F4 series. The wrong AF number means
the UART initialises but nothing comes out of the pin.

Baud rate register (BRR):
Calculated from peripheral clock frequency divided by desired baud rate.
Using the wrong clock source, APB1 versus APB2, produces a baud rate wrong by
exactly a factor of 2.

Word length, stop bits, parity:
Must match exactly on both ends of the connection.

TE and RE bits:
Transmitter enable and receiver enable in the CR1 register. Forgetting either
means one direction simply does not work with no error indication.

NVIC configuration:
If using interrupts, the USART interrupt request must be enabled in the NVIC and
priority must be set. Without this the interrupt handler never fires.

Most common first-try mistakes:

Wrong GPIO alternate function number:
The code looks correct but the pin does nothing. Always verify the AF number in
the MCU datasheet alternate function table, not just the reference manual.

APB1 versus APB2 clock confusion:
USART1 and USART6 are on APB2. USART2, USART3 are on APB1.
Using the wrong clock source makes the baud rate exactly 2x wrong, which looks
like a baud rate mismatch problem.

Forgetting to enable peripheral clock:
Registers appear to exist but all writes are ignored silently. No error is reported.

TX pin not configured as push-pull output:
If configured as input or open-drain, the signal never drives HIGH and only LOW
transitions are visible on the line.

Misreading the alternate function table:
TX and RX on the same pin number but different ports. For example, PA9 and PB9
look similar in a table but connect to completely different signals.


Q2 - Stepper Motors and Drivers
---------------------------------
What is microstepping and what are its trade-offs? At what point does increasing
microstepping stop improving motion quality, and why?

ANSWER:

What microstepping is:
Full step means the motor moves one full tooth pitch per pulse. Microstepping divides
each full step into smaller increments by energising both coils simultaneously at
varying current ratios. This creates intermediate positions between full steps.

Steps per revolution for a standard 200-step motor:
Full step:  200 steps,  1.8 degrees per step
1/8 step:   1600 steps, 0.225 degrees per step
1/16 step:  3200 steps, 0.1125 degrees per step
1/256 step: 51200 steps, 0.007 degrees per step

Trade-offs:

Higher microstepping gives smoother motion and less vibration, but holding torque
drops significantly above 1/16 microstepping. More pulses are required for the same
distance, increasing CPU load. Positional resolution improves in theory but not in
practice beyond the motor's mechanical tolerance.

Where it stops helping:

Beyond 1/16 or 1/32 microstepping, real-world improvements stop because:

The mechanical tolerance of the motor itself, including shaft wobble and tooth
irregularity, is typically plus or minus 5 percent of a full step. No amount of
finer microstepping can overcome this physical limitation.

Load torque variation overwhelms the very small holding force of a high microstep.
The motor snaps to the nearest full or half step under any real load.

Intermediate positions are only accurate when the motor is completely stationary
and unloaded.

This is why TMC drivers use 256 microsteps internally for smoothness but interpolate
from whatever external step rate is commanded. The motion feels smooth without
incurring the torque penalty of true 256 microstepping.


Q3 - TMC Drivers
-----------------
What is the advantage of running TMC stepper drivers in UART mode over standalone
mode? What can you configure over UART that you cannot in standalone?

ANSWER:

Standalone mode:
Configured entirely by physical pins (CFG0, CFG1, CFG2). Limited to a small number
of preset combinations of current and chopper settings. The configuration is fixed
at the hardware level and cannot be changed without physically moving jumpers.

UART mode allows configuration of:

Run current (IRUN):
Set the exact RMS current in milliamps. Not just a preset level but a precise value
matched to the specific motor.

Hold current (IHOLD):
Reduce current when the motor is stationary to decrease heat generation.

Hold delay (IHOLDDELAY):
Configure how long after a move stops before current reduces to the hold level.

StealthChop speed threshold:
Switch automatically between silent StealthChop mode and high-torque SpreadCycle
mode at a configurable speed threshold.

StallGuard sensitivity:
Tune the sensorless homing detection threshold for the specific motor, load, and
speed. Cannot be adjusted at all in standalone mode.

CoolStep threshold:
Dynamic current reduction under light load, saving power and reducing heat.

Chopper timing settings:
Fine-tune for the inductance of the specific motor being used.

Driver status readback (most important):
Read driver temperature, stall detection flags, short-circuit flags, and actual
motor current in real time. This is completely impossible in standalone mode.
In standalone mode, the driver can be overheating or losing sync with no indication.
In UART mode, polling the driver status gives full visibility into what is happening.


Q4 - PCB Layout
----------------
You are laying out a PCB that has stepper motor drivers, an STM32, and analog
temperature sensors all on the same board. What are your layout priorities and why?

ANSWER:

Priority 1: Stepper driver placement and thermal management
Place drivers at the edge of the board for airflow access. The thermal pad on the
underside of the driver package must connect to a large copper pour with at least
8 to 10 thermal vias to inner and back copper layers. Stepper drivers are the
highest power components on the board and thermal failure is the most common
board failure mode in motion control systems.

Priority 2: Analog sensor area isolation
Place thermistor inputs, ADC reference, and analog filter components in a dedicated
corner of the board. No high-frequency traces should run near or underneath this
area. The analog ground should be a quiet island connected to the main ground plane
at exactly one point to prevent digital switching noise from coupling in.

Priority 3: Decoupling capacitors
Place a 100nF ceramic capacitor on every VDD pin of the STM32, within 1mm physically.
Stepper drivers need bulk capacitance of at least 100uF on their motor voltage input,
placed right at the VMOT pin to handle the switching current spikes.

Priority 4: High-current path routing
Motor coil traces carry 1 to 3 amps. Use a trace width calculator. At minimum 1mm
width for 2A on 1oz copper. Keep these traces short and do not route them near analog
or signal lines. Use a star ground connection for motor return currents back to the
main ground point.

Priority 5: Crystal and clock circuit
Place the crystal and its load capacitors as close to the MCU as physically possible.
No other traces should pass underneath the crystal circuit as this introduces noise
and can cause clock instability.

Priority 6: Step and direction signal routing
Keep step and direction signal traces away from motor coil traces. Motor coil lines
switch at high current and couple noise into adjacent signal traces, causing false
steps or direction changes.


Q5 - Sensor Integration
------------------------
You are reading a thermistor with an ADC. Your readings are noisy and drift
slightly over time. What are the possible hardware and firmware causes and
how do you fix each?

ANSWER:

Hardware causes and fixes:

No filtering capacitor on ADC input:
Add a 100nF ceramic capacitor from the ADC input pin to GND, placed right at the pin.
This forms a low-pass filter with the source resistance and removes high-frequency noise.

VREF+ noise:
Decouple the ADC voltage reference with a 100nF capacitor and a 10uF capacitor close
to the VREF pin. A noisy reference directly modulates every ADC reading.

Thermistor supply rail noise:
Power the thermistor voltage divider from a filtered 3.3V rail, not directly from
a noisy supply. Use an RC filter on the supply to the divider if needed.

Long cable picking up EMI:
Add an RC low-pass filter at the board entry point where the cable connects.
1 kilohm series resistance and 100nF to GND is a good starting point.

Ground reference mismatch:
Ensure the thermistor ground and the ADC ground are the same physical node.
Any resistance in the ground path adds an offset error proportional to current.

Self-heating of the thermistor:
The excitation current through the thermistor heats it slightly, causing a reading
error that increases over time. Use a higher resistance thermistor to reduce excitation
current, or use a pulsed excitation approach.

Firmware causes and fixes:

No oversampling:
Average 8 to 16 ADC readings before reporting a value. This reduces random noise
by the square root of the number of samples.

ADC reading during switching events:
Trigger ADC conversions only when PWM outputs are in a known state, not during
switching transitions. Synchronise ADC timing to PWM period.

Integer truncation error:
Accumulate the sum in a 32-bit variable before dividing by the number of samples.
Dividing a 12-bit value by 16 using 16-bit integer math loses precision.

Wrong Steinhart-Hart or Beta coefficients:
Verify thermistor coefficients against the datasheet. Measure the thermistor at two
or three known temperatures and compare to expected values.

Note on drift specifically:
Drift that increases slowly over time, independent of noise, is almost always caused
by self-heating of the thermistor from its own excitation current, or thermal drift
of the resistors in the voltage divider. Use 1 percent tolerance resistors minimum.


Q6 - MOSFETs and Switching
---------------------------
You are using a MOSFET to PWM-control a resistive heater at 10Hz. Explain how you
would select the MOSFET and what would change in your selection if the frequency
was 50kHz instead.

ANSWER:

At 10Hz:
The MOSFET switches only 10 times per second. Switching losses are completely
negligible. The MOSFET spends almost all its time either fully on or fully off.

Selection criteria at 10Hz:

Rds(on): conduction loss equals I squared times Rds(on). At low frequency this
is the only significant loss. Select the lowest Rds(on) available in the package.

Logic-level gate drive: ensure Vgs(th) is compatible with your drive voltage.
For a 3.3V GPIO, use a logic-level MOSFET such as IRLZ44N with Vgs(th) below 2V.
A standard MOSFET with Vgs(th) of 4V will not fully turn on from 3.3V.

Continuous current rating: must exceed the heater current with reasonable margin.

Voltage rating: must exceed the supply voltage, typically with 2x margin.

At 50kHz, everything changes:
The MOSFET now switches 50,000 times per second. Switching losses dominate.

Gate charge (Qg):
Every switching cycle, the gate capacitance must be charged and discharged.
This takes current from the gate driver. High Qg means high switching loss and
a hot gate driver. Select low Qg.

Rise and fall time:
Slow transitions mean the MOSFET spends time in the linear region where both
current and voltage are high simultaneously. This creates high instantaneous
power dissipation. Select fast switching devices.

Output capacitance (Coss):
Charged and discharged every cycle. Contributes directly to switching loss.
Select low Coss.

Gate driver:
At 50kHz a direct GPIO cannot supply gate current fast enough. Use a dedicated
gate driver IC between the GPIO and the MOSFET gate. The driver must supply
peak current equal to Qg divided by the desired transition time.

PCB layout:
At 50kHz, layout becomes critical. Keep the gate drive loop and power switching
loop as small as possible. Parasitic inductance in the loop causes voltage spikes
at switching that can destroy the MOSFET.


Q7 - Serial Communication
--------------------------
What is the fundamental difference between UART, SPI, and I2C? For each one,
give a real example of a component or use case where that protocol is the right choice.

ANSWER:

UART (Universal Asynchronous Receiver Transmitter):
Asynchronous, meaning each device has its own clock. No shared clock line.
Baud rate must be configured identically on both ends.
Typically point-to-point only (one device to one device).
Two wires: TX and RX. Full duplex.
Speed: typically up to 5 Mbps in practice.

Real use case: GPS module, Bluetooth module (HC-05), debug console output,
communication between two microcontrollers, TMC2209 stepper driver configuration.
Best choice when you need simple point-to-point communication at moderate speed
and do not want to deal with addressing or a shared bus.

SPI (Serial Peripheral Interface):
Synchronous. Master provides the clock signal.
Separate chip select line per device. Each device needs its own CS pin on the master.
Four wires: MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS. Full duplex.
Speed: up to 50 Mbps or higher depending on device.

Real use case: SD card, SPI flash memory (W25Q64), TFT display, high-speed DAC or ADC.
Best choice when you need fast data transfer and have the GPIO pins available.
SD cards physically cannot use any other protocol.

I2C (Inter-Integrated Circuit):
Synchronous. Master provides clock on SCL line.
Multiple devices share the same two wires using unique 7-bit addresses.
Up to 127 devices on one bus.
Two wires: SDA and SCL. Half duplex, bidirectional.
Speed: 100kHz standard, 400kHz fast mode, 1MHz fast-plus mode.
Requires pull-up resistors on both lines.

Real use case: OLED display, BMP280 temperature and pressure sensor, EEPROM (AT24C32),
RTC module (DS3231), multiple sensors sharing the same two wires.
Best choice when you have many slow peripherals and want to minimise pin count.


Q8 - Case Study
----------------
A stepper motor axis homes correctly and moves accurately for the first 10 minutes,
then starts losing steps, getting worse over time until the machine is restarted.
No firmware changes were made. What are your top 5 suspects and how do you test each?

ANSWER:

The key pattern is: works correctly, then degrades progressively, and restarts fix it.
This points to a thermal or accumulation problem, not a random fault.

Suspect 1: Stepper driver overheating (most likely)
As driver temperature rises, the thermal protection circuit reduces current output
to protect the device. Torque drops and steps are lost. Gets progressively worse
as temperature climbs toward the protection threshold.
Test: measure driver surface temperature with an IR thermometer after 10 minutes
of operation. In UART mode, read the IRUN register and driver status flags to check
if current is being reduced automatically.
Fix: improve heatsinking, increase airflow over the driver, reduce IRUN by 10 percent.

Suspect 2: Motor winding overheating
Motor winding resistance increases with temperature. The same supply voltage drives
less current through hotter windings. Less current means less torque. Steps are lost
against the load.
Test: measure motor body temperature after 10 minutes. Calculate what the current
would be at the elevated winding temperature compared to cold.
Fix: reduce hold current (IHOLD) to prevent the motor staying at full current
when stationary. Improve airflow around the motor.

Suspect 3: Mechanical binding increasing with temperature
Thermal expansion of the frame, leadscrew, or linear rails causes friction to increase
as the machine warms up. The motor must work harder against increasing mechanical load
until it cannot keep up.
Test: turn the axis by hand when cold and again after 10 minutes running.
Check if resistance is greater when warm. Inspect axis alignment after running.
Fix: adjust eccentric nuts, check belt tension or leadscrew nut preload, lubricate.

Suspect 4: Power supply voltage sag
The PSU heats up under sustained load and its output voltage drops slightly.
Motor drivers receive less voltage, leaving less headroom for current regulation.
Steps are lost under load conditions that were fine when the PSU was cool.
Test: measure PSU output voltage at cold start and again after 10 minutes with
all loads running. Any drop greater than 0.5V is significant.
Fix: replace or upgrade the PSU. Add bulk electrolytic capacitance near the motor
drivers to buffer momentary current demands.

Suspect 5: Firmware timer drift or interrupt starvation
Under sustained operation, a background process accumulates execution time and
begins delaying step pulse generation. Pulses arrive too slowly for the commanded
speed and the motor stalls.
Test: check whether the problem occurs at all speeds equally or only above a
certain speed. If only at higher speeds, the step rate is being throttled by
CPU load. Scope the step pulse timing cold versus after 10 minutes.
Fix: increase the step timer interrupt priority so it cannot be delayed by lower
priority tasks. Profile background interrupt load and reduce unnecessary processing.
